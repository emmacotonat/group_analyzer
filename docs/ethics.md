# Ethics and Privacy

Network analysis can reveal sensitive information. A sociogram is not just a visualisation; it is a representation of social positions, recognition, distance and possible vulnerability.

---

## Principles

1. **Informed consent**: participants should know that relational data will be analysed.
2. **Data minimisation**: only collect attributes that are necessary for the research question.
3. **Anonymisation**: use pseudonyms or participant IDs in public outputs.
4. **Non-stigmatisation**: do not label people as problematic, excluded or powerful without context.
5. **Interpretive caution**: centrality is not moral value; periphery is not personal failure.
6. **Right not to answer**: conflict and avoidance questions must be optional.
7. **Contextual interpretation**: complement the sociogram with qualitative evidence.

---

## Sensitive outputs

Be especially careful with:

- conflict nominations
- avoidance nominations
- isolated nodes
- low belonging or safety scores
- high negative received nominations
- identifiable small groups
- screenshots of interactive graphs

---

## Recommended anonymisation workflow

1. Keep a private file linking names to participant IDs.
2. Use `participant_id` or pseudonyms in the analysis file.
3. Do not upload real identifiable data to GitHub.
4. Share only aggregated or anonymised figures in the TFG.
5. Remove raw open-ended answers if they contain identifiable details.

---

## Suggested ethics paragraph for the TFG

> Because sociograms can expose sensitive relational positions, this study treats network data as confidential and interpretive. The objective is not to classify participants morally, but to analyse how recognition, support, influence, cohesion and invisibility are distributed in a group. The analysis therefore combines computational metrics with sociological caution, anonymisation and contextual interpretation.
